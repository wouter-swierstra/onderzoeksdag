Abstracts

Algorithmic Flowers - Han Hoogeveen

In this talk I will discuss a problem that plays an important role in the flower industry: we must determine how many mother plants are required to be able to produce a given demand of cuttings. This sounds like an easy problem, but working with living material (plants) introduces complications that are rarely encountered in optimization problems: the constraints for cutting such that the mother plant remains in shape are not explicitly known.

We have tackled this problem by a combination of data mining and linear programming. We apply data mining to infer constraints that a cutting pattern, stating how many cuttings to harvest in each period, should obey, and we use these constraints in a linear programming formulation that determines the minimum number of mother plants necessary. We then consider the problem of maximizing the total profit given the number of mother plants and show how to solve it through linear programming.

Domain-specific Type Error Diagnosis - Jurriaan Hage

Domain-specific languages (DSLs) are (often restricted) programming languages suited for a particular domain. In software technology and industry they have become increasingly popular because programs in the given domain can be extremely concise and clear, and can often be written by domain-experts who are not trained programmers.

To avoid the effort of developing a tool chain (a compiler, an IDE, and libraries) for every DSL, and to ease the integration of multiple DSLs, an alternative way of implementing DSLs is to embed them in a suitably powerful general purpose language, like C++ or Haskell. In that case, a DSL is often simply a library that exhibits the ``fluency’’ of the domain and that is encoded in some way in the general purpose language.

The complication that I address in this talk is how to manipulate the type error messages that are given by the compiler, so that they are phrased in terms of the domain the programmer understands without revealing the underlying encoding that a programmer should not have to know about.

Exceptional Model Mining - Ad Feelders

Exceptional Model Mining (EMM) is an exploratory data mining technique that has been developed in recent years in collaboration between Leiden and Utrecht. We explain the general idea of EMM and illustrate its application through examples.

Music Information Retrieval: integrating musical information from multiple sources - Vincent Koops

In this talk, we present our current research on the fundamental issue of integrating heterogeneous information from multiple, autonomous data sources using data fusion, and its application in Music Information Retrieval. The recent explosion of crowd-sourced information, such as on the internet, calls for sophisticated algorithms for data-driven quality assessment and data integration to create better, and more reliable data. In Music Information Retrieval, a typical application scenario are chord label sequences. We present our approach to integrate heterogeneous chord label sequences using data fusion, in order to create significantly better chord label sequences, demonstrating our ongoing collaboration with Chordify, an online music e-learning platform. The Chordify website teaches users the chords needed to play along with music from websites such as YouTube. Chords are a compact way to describe the harmony of a musical piece, and sequences of chords are often used by guitarists and pianists to play a piece. Chordify allows users to create their own, personalized chord sequences for a song if they disagree with the output of the Chordify automatic chord extraction algorithm. Due to the many users of Chordify, this results in large amounts of crowd-sourced musical information. We demonstrate how we can integrate these heterogeneous chord label sequences using data fusion, to create significantly better chord label sequences. Since data fusion is a very generic approach, we also show how we apply it to improving the state assurance in machine state prediction from audio sources for the specific case of quadcopters, in an ongoing collaboration with Carnegie Mellon University. Furthermore, we show our current research aiming to model the musical preference of a large group of Chordify users. Using deep learning, we try to move from a normative to a more descriptive automatic chord extraction method.

SleepCare: persuasive strategies in e-coaching for insomnia

Improving assignment adherence is a major challenge in all types of behavior therapies (BT), in particular in self-help therapies from books and internet sites. In the Sleepcare-project, methods from persuasive technology were investigated to improve adherence of self-help BT for insomnia (BT-i). The project aimed at a generic coaching system that integrates persuasive strategies with protocols for behavior change in BT-i, and the technology that supports the system. The interface is based on the so-called Talk-and-Tools metaphor where a clear distinction is made between conversational interaction (‘Talk’)and direct manipulation (‘Tools’). BT-i protocols and persuasive strategies, such as notification and commitment, were implemented in a fully automated smartphone application and integrated in a research infrastructure for empirical testing. In this presentation, I will give a brief overview of the development of the system, the rationale behind its design, some preliminary results from a Randomized Control Trial and our future plans.

Directional Field Synthesis, Design, and Processing

Directional fields on discrete surfaces and in volumes are key components of geometry processing. Many applications make use of such fields, among which are remeshing, surface parametrization (texture mapping), texture synthesis, fluid simulation, and many more. I will present the challenges and the limitations in the design and the analysis of such fields, and focus on novel ways to compute them.
